Internet-Based Social Connections of Black American College Students in Pre–COVID-19 and Peri–COVID-19 Pandemic Periods: Network Analysis

Eun Lee, Heejun Kim, Yildiz Esener, Terika McCall
Journal of Medical Internet Research, 26(1), e55531

Abstract

The COVID-19 pandemic significantly disrupted social connections and communication patterns worldwide, with potentially unique impacts on minority communities. This study examines how the internet-based social connections of Black American college students changed during the pandemic, providing insights into the resilience and adaptation of these networks during a major social disruption.

Using network analysis methods, we compared the structure, density, and characteristics of online social networks among Black American college students before and during the COVID-19 pandemic. Our analysis revealed significant shifts in connection patterns, information flow, and community structure as students adapted to pandemic conditions.

Key findings include changes in network centrality measures, shifts in community clustering, and alterations in the types of information shared across these networks. We observed both contraction in some aspects of network connectivity and expansion in others, suggesting complex adaptation mechanisms. The study highlights how digital platforms served as crucial infrastructure for maintaining social connections during physical distancing, while also revealing disparities in digital access and engagement.

These findings have implications for understanding the resilience of minority community networks during crises and can inform interventions to support social connectivity during future disruptions. The research also contributes to our understanding of how digital technologies mediate social relationships in challenging circumstances.
