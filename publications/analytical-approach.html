Analytical approach to the generalized friendship paradox in networks with correlated attributes

HH Jo, E Lee, YH Eom
Physical Review E 104 (5), 054301, 2021/11/5

Abstract

The friendship paradox (FP) states that your friends have, on average, more friends than you do. This phenomenon has been generalized to attributes other than the number of friends, leading to the generalized friendship paradox (GFP). The GFP has profound implications for how individuals perceive themselves and others in social networks, potentially leading to widespread feelings of inferiority when people compare themselves to their seemingly more successful or popular friends.

In this paper, we develop an analytical approach to understand the GFP in networks where node attributes are correlated. We derive exact expressions for the magnitude of the GFP in terms of network structure and attribute correlations. Our analysis reveals how the correlation between different attributes, such as popularity and wealth, can amplify or diminish the paradox effect.

We validate our analytical results using both synthetic and empirical network data, showing excellent agreement between theory and observations. Our findings provide insights into how network structure and attribute correlations contribute to social perception biases, with implications for understanding social comparison processes and their effects on individual well-being in connected societies.
