Content and Social Network Analyses of Depression-related Tweets of African American College Students

T Mccall, H Kim, E Lee, A Lakdawala, CS Bolton Iii
Proceedings of the 54th Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences, 2597, 2021/1/5

Abstract

Mental health issues, particularly depression, are prevalent among college students, with unique manifestations and challenges in minority populations. Social media platforms like Twitter provide valuable insights into how students express and discuss mental health concerns. This study examines depression-related tweets from African American college students, applying both content analysis and social network analysis to understand communication patterns and themes.

Our research collected and analyzed tweets containing depression-related keywords from a sample of African American college students. Using natural language processing techniques, we identified key themes, sentiment patterns, and linguistic markers associated with depressive expressions. Concurrently, we mapped the social network structure of interactions around these tweets to understand how depression-related content flows through these communities.

The content analysis revealed several prominent themes, including academic stress, social isolation, stigma around mental health, and coping mechanisms. The network analysis identified influential nodes in the conversation, patterns of information dissemination, and community structures that form around depression-related discussions. We observed that certain types of content received more engagement and that specific network positions were associated with different types of depression-related expressions.

These findings provide insights into how depression manifests in the digital expressions of African American college students and how social networks mediate these expressions. The research has implications for developing culturally sensitive mental health interventions, reducing stigma, and leveraging social media for mental health support in minority college populations.
