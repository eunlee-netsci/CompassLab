Social contagion with degree-dependent thresholds

E Lee, P Holme
Physical Review E 96 (1), 012315, 2017/7/17

Abstract

Social contagion—the spread of behaviors, ideas, or information through social networks—is a fundamental process in human societies. Traditional threshold models of social contagion assume that an individual's adoption threshold is independent of their social connectivity. However, empirical evidence suggests that highly connected individuals may be either more or less susceptible to social influence, depending on the context.

In this study, we investigate how the relationship between an individual's network degree and their adoption threshold affects the dynamics of social contagion. We develop a model where the threshold for adoption can either increase or decrease as a function of the number of social connections. Through extensive numerical simulations on various network structures, we characterize how this degree-threshold relationship influences the speed, reach, and patterns of contagion.

Our findings reveal that when thresholds decrease with degree (highly connected individuals are more easily influenced), contagion spreads more rapidly and reaches a larger fraction of the population. Conversely, when thresholds increase with degree (highly connected individuals are more resistant to influence), contagion tends to be slower and more limited in scope. We also identify critical threshold-degree relationships that lead to cascading behaviors and tipping points in the contagion process.

These results provide insights into how the heterogeneity of influence thresholds across differently connected individuals shapes collective social dynamics, with implications for understanding phenomena ranging from the adoption of innovations to the spread of health behaviors and political movements.
